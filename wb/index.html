The Heartland, or some other name

Since the early 2010s—I can't recall for certain but it would have been some time in late 2012 or early 2013—I have been working on a fantasy setting which has no particular name. If necessary I have referred to it as The Heartland, the name of the "main" region in the larger world which I tend to focus on, and the reader may use this name if they really want to. My vision for this setting has changed substantially over the years, and this hub is meant as a place for me to document the details of the setting, and to share it with anyone who might be interested. There are tons of notes and various tidbits of information spread around various places after over ten years of work, so the process of getting things on here may be somewhat slow.

About the Heartland setting

The philosophy of its worldbuilding

A name for the genre of the setting of the Heartland may be alien fantasy, a term I have used in the past when discussing it with others.

Since J.R.R. Tolkien's The Lord of the Rings exploded in popularity in the sixties, much of contemporary fantasy in the west has been defined by the archetypes and mold that he initially set up: humans, elves, dwarves, orcs, great magic power, dark overlords, aged wizards, and so on. This isn't to denigrate or malign the work of Tolkien: the depth of his setting is something to aspire to, and the amount of work he put into it is both admirable and almost unimaginable—his Legendarium was a work that he spent much of his adult life on. But his influence on fantasy always looming in the background can, to some degree, grow tiring to see so much repetition built off of the work of one man.

Though I was a bit too young to really wrap my head around the lore, a large chunk of my preteens and early teens were spent playing The Elder Scrolls III: Morrowind. The unique and enthralling atmosphere of Morrowind has had an indelible effect on my worldbuilding to the point that it is likely the second-most important influence on me, after Tolkien and his Legendarium. But, as with Tolkien, this influence is indirect: I am more interested in replicating how Morrowind made me feel (and continues to make me feel, to this day) as I wander around the game world, rather than trying to build off of Morrowind.

Although I had arrived at the philosophy on my own, I've found that the Tamriel Rebuilt captures one of my guiding principles in lore in a concise way:

Principle of Alienity – What is boring and mundane is wrong; what is interesting and alien is right.

[But] alien in excess is mundane.

—Five principles (and three sub-principles) of lore, Tamriel Rebuilt
